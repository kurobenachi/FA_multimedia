The Significance of Kanbun Education in Japan:
A Comparative Analysis of Classical Education

1. Introduction

Classical education plays an important role in shaping a nation's cultural identity and political cohesion. While many countries have elective courses in foreign classical languages such as Latin, and ancient Greek, not many countries have mandatory courses. Classical education, particularly in the form of Kanbun in Japan, not only preserves linguistic heritage but also reinforces national identity, which is a critical aspect of political stability and societal integration. The teaching of Kanbun, rooted in Japan’s historical and cultural exchanges with China, highlights how education systems can serve as tools for political socialization, instilling shared values and cultural continuity among citizens.

Although classical Chinese, called Kanbun, has long been an integral part of education in Japan as compulsory, its importance has sometimes been criticized. However, from a political perspective, Kanbun education can be seen as a means of fostering a collective cultural identity and a sense of belonging, which are essential for a cohesive and stable society. Various papers have mentioned the uniqueness of Kanbun teaching methods, such as the Kundoku system of reconstructing Classical Chinese into Japanese, which not only demonstrates linguistic innovation but also reflects the political and cultural adaptations that have shaped Japanese society over centuries.

This paper aims to emphasize the necessity of Kanbun education in Japan by drawing comparisons with classical language education in other countries. By examining the political implications of classical education in shaping national identity and promoting cultural continuity, the discussion will provide insights into why Kanbun remains relevant today. The first section introduces the current educational situation in Japan. The following sections will illustrate the situation of some European and East Asian countries and their backgrounds, a comparison of each country, and a discussion of the importance of Kanbun education.

2. Classical foreign language education in Japan

The reception of Classical Chinese in Japan has a rich history dating back more than a millennium. Ab initio, Kanji was gradually adopted into Japanese culture from the third to the seventh century along with the visit of ‘Toraijin’; henceforth influenced by Chinese culture, Japan adopted Classical Chinese as its scholarly language and used it for Japanese literature (Takeo & Maruyama, 2007). In the middle of the Heian period, ‘Kanbun Kundoku’, the system of transforming classical Chinese into Japanese, developed and caused the influx of Chinese vocabulary, which had a considerable influence on the Japanese language through quotations from classical Chinese literature (Sato, 2003). After that, classical Chinese, called ‘Kanbun’, has continuously played an important role in Japanese literature.

At present, Kanbun remains an integral part of the Japanese educational system. It is typically taught at the secondary level and focuses on the appreciation of Japanese culture using ‘Kanbun Kundoku’. Students study a wide range of topics, including ancient literature, poetry, and historical documents, some of which are the source of Japanese historical idioms and vocabulary.

According to the Curriculum Guidelines (2017), the purpose of classical education in Japan is multifaceted. It serves to develop the ability to think and imagine through the classics of predecessors, and to deepen the understanding of the nation’s traditional language and culture. It also serves to deepen students’ awareness of themselves as bearers of Japanese language and culture, and to cultivate an attitude of relating to others and society through language.

However, classical Chinese education in Japan has long been criticized. Criticism of Kanbun education dates back to the Meiji period and is represented by Hisoka Maejima, known as the ‘Father of the Postal System’. He pointed out that Kanbun and Kanji had academic difficulties in spreading national education (Masuda, 2013). Considering that the beginning of Kanbun as a compulsory education also began in the Meiji period, the history of Kanbun education cannot be told without criticism. Nowadays, some argue that classical education deprives students of time to study subjects that have a more practical use, such as English and economics in the globalized world. While this opinion has received a certain amount of support on the Internet, there are still significant dissenting voices.

3. Classical foreign language education Abroad

This section refers to the teaching of Chinese literature in South Korea and Latin in Europe. The reason for this choice is that the historical backgrounds of both regions are similar in that ancient superpowers have greatly influenced the cultures of their regions. In addition, there are no examples of classical foreign languages being actively taught in secondary education other than Chinese literature, Latin, and classical Greek, and classical Greek and Latin are taught in the same European region, and Latin has a more dominant position in education because of Christianity.

In South Korea, classical Chinese is taught as a compulsory subject in secondary education, and the method of ‘Gugyeol’, which is similar to Japanese ‘Kanbun Kundoku’, is used to read classical Chinese as Korean. However, it is treated as a foreign language subject, in contrast to the Japanese situation, where classical Chinese is regarded as a part of its linguistic culture (Kim, 2013).

Korea has long been a tributary state of the Chinese empire and, like Japan, has embraced Chinese culture, represented by Buddhism and Confucianism. The degree of acceptance is much higher than in Japan because of the Korean characteristics of geographical distance from China and tribute. As an indication of this, the creation of Chinese literature called Hanmun has been active in Korea, and as many works written by Koreans have been adopted as teaching materials for Chinese literature as those written in China (Kim, 2013).

However, with the end of the subservient relationship with China at the end of the 19th century and the emergence of opposition to China, Hangeul, which had been invented in the 15th century and was considered inferior to Chinese characters, began to replace Chinese characters, and strengthen its position. However, the Japanese rule in Korea led to the use of both Chinese characters and Hangeul in Korea due to the influence of the Japanese writing system. On the other hand, as a reaction to Chinese culture and Japanese rule, Korea eventually promoted the independent use of Hangeul (Kim, 2013).

This historical background has had a significant impact on the education of Hanmun in Korea today. Although the Hangul-only theory is completely dominant in Korea, conservatives, strongly influenced by Confucianism, promote the idea of the combined use of Chinese characters and Hangul. Today, Koreans recognise Hanmun as a foreign language, but with the intention of recognising Hanmun as part of the national language culture, conservatives have chosen Hanmun texts written by Koreans as their teaching materials (Kim, 2013).

Through the overview of the current situation of Latin education in Europe, a great difference between East Asia and Europe in the education of the classical literature of the ancient superpowers is revealed. In Italy, 76% of secondary school students study Latin, followed by 34% in Austria, 32% in Germany, 24% in Flemish Belgium, 13% in France, and only 2.5% in Poland (Malinowski, 2016). In Croatia, since the learning of foreign languages is considered very important, two years of compulsory Latin are generally required in secondary education (Prpić, 2009).

According to Posner and Sala (2023), Latin instruction in Europe has a rich and extensive history that spans many centuries. Its origins can be traced back to ancient Rome, where Latin was the language of the Roman Empire. During this time, Latin served as the lingua franca of Europe, uniting diverse regions under a common language.

As the Roman Empire began to decline, Latin continued to be used by the Catholic Church as the language of religious texts and liturgy. The medieval period saw Latin being taught in monastic schools and cathedral schools, where it was considered essential for clergy and scholars. However, in the Renaissance, Latin's everyday use declined with the rise of vernacular languages.

Thenceforth, with the rise of vernacular languages and the spread of nationalism, Latin's dominance started to wane. The later period witnessed a gradual decline in the teaching of Latin in schools, as modern languages and sciences gained prominence. Today, Latin instruction is primarily limited to specialized schools and universities. While no longer a widespread subject, Latin continues to have a significant influence on scientific, legal, and theological terminology.

4. Comparison and Discussion

By comparing these different approaches, the uniqueness of Japanese classical Chinese education can be seen. This uniqueness comes from treating it as a part of Japanese language culture. By reading Kanbun using the Kundoku method, students can better understand the cultural history of both Japanese and Chinese, including cultural exchanges in East Asian history. It is an integrated approach that combines language skills, literary appreciation, and cultural understanding in one package.

Promoting this type of education in Japan can be beneficial in many ways. First, it enables students to understand their cultural roots and their deep connection to Chinese culture. Second, it allows students to develop their language skills in a way that broadens their perspectives. Finally, it encourages students to engage in critical thinking and interpretation, as the translation of classical texts is a complex and nuanced process. Japan must therefore continue to value and promote this traditional approach to education.

5. Conclusion

In conclusion, Japan's unique approach to compulsory Kanbun education, particularly the teaching of classical Chinese, has had a far-reaching influence on the Japanese language and literature and on the country's cultural heritage. Comparisons with South Korea and Europe underline the distinctive national influences on the practice of classical language teaching.

While there are criticisms, particularly about the practicality of Kanbun, it is important to recognize its value in promoting reading comprehension and cultural understanding. It is also important to maintain a balance between preserving heritage and preparing students for a globalized world. The complexity of this issue invites further research and discussion.

Despite the challenges, Kanbun education, with its multi-dimensional benefits, provides a valuable case study for incorporating historical culture into modern education. It is therefore worth continuing to promote this tradition, adapting it as necessary to contemporary needs. The study of Kanbun offers an insightful perspective on the intersection of past and present in educational practice.
